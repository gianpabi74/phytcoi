# Purpose

This document defines the Planning Cycle of PHYTCOI.

The Planning Cycle describes how the organism transforms cognition into candidate future actions.

The Planning Cycle does not execute actions.

The Planning Cycle does not approve actions.

The Planning Cycle does not modify reality.

Its purpose is to reason about possible futures while preserving Habitat First governance.

The Planning Cycle is the first bridge between cognition and future operational behavior.

Without planning, cognition remains passive.

Without governance, planning becomes dangerous.

Therefore planning exists between cognition and execution.

---

# Why Planning Exists

Observation explains reality.

Understanding explains meaning.

Truth explains validated knowledge.

Risk explains potential impact.

None of these answer:

```text
What should be considered next?
```

Planning exists to answer that question.

Planning is the process of exploring possible futures before they happen.

The Planner allows PHYTCOI to evaluate multiple possible paths without committing to any of them.

---


The Planning Cycle consumes both cognition and governance.

It produces candidate futures.

# Planning Ontology

The Planner does not think in terms of products.

The Planner does not think in terms of hostnames.

The Planner does not think in terms of vendors.

The Planner thinks in terms of:

```text
Functions

Capabilities

Domains

Dependencies

Risks

Objectives

Constraints
```

This principle is mandatory.

A portable organism must plan using universal concepts.

---

# Definition of a Plan

A plan is a structured description of a possible future intervention.

A plan is neither true nor false.

A plan is neither approved nor denied.

A plan represents a hypothesis about a future state.

Example:

```text
Current state:
identity capability degradation risk exists

Possible future:
restore identity capability availability

Plan:
candidate intervention path
```

The plan itself changes nothing.

---

# Planning Lifecycle

Every plan passes through a cognitive lifecycle.

```text
Observation
    ↓
Need Detection
    ↓
Objective Formation
    ↓
Candidate Generation
    ↓
Candidate Evaluation
    ↓
Candidate Ranking
    ↓
Recommendation
    ↓
Review
```

Execution is not part of the planning lifecycle.

---

# Stage 1 — Observation

Planning begins with observation.

The Planner does not create needs.

The Planner discovers needs from cognition.

Examples:

```text
Observed degradation

Observed instability

Observed uncertainty

Observed dependency risk

Observed missing capability
```

Observation is the source of planning.

---

# Stage 2 — Need Detection

The Planner attempts to determine whether a meaningful need exists.

Examples:

```text
Availability maintenance need

Recovery maintenance need

Identity maintenance need

Resolution maintenance need

Continuity maintenance need
```

Not every observation creates a need.

Need formation requires supporting cognition.

---

# Stage 3 — Objective Formation

Needs become objectives.

Examples:

```text
Maintain availability

Reduce uncertainty

Improve resilience

Preserve continuity

Protect habitat
```

Objectives are intentionally abstract.

Objectives describe desired outcomes.

They do not describe implementation.

---

# Stage 4 — Candidate Generation

Once an objective exists, the Planner generates possible approaches.

Multiple candidates should be preferred.

Example:

```text
Candidate A

Candidate B

Candidate C
```

The Planner should avoid single-path thinking whenever possible.

Multiple futures improve decision quality.

---

# Stage 5 — Candidate Evaluation

Each candidate must be evaluated.

Evaluation dimensions may include:

```text
Expected benefit

Expected risk

Expected complexity

Expected reversibility

Expected confidence

Expected dependency impact
```

Evaluation must remain evidence-based.

---

# Stage 6 — Candidate Ranking

Candidates are ranked according to Planner objectives.

The ranking process should prefer:

```text
Higher safety

Higher reversibility

Lower uncertainty

Lower habitat impact
```

Optimization alone is insufficient.

Safety remains dominant.

---

# Stage 7 — Recommendation

The Planner produces recommendations.

Recommendations may include:

```text
Recommended candidate

Alternative candidates

Known risks

Known assumptions

Known unknowns
```

Recommendations remain informational.

---

# Stage 8 — Review

Recommendations enter governance review.

Review may result in:

```text
Acceptance

Rejection

Delay

Request for more evidence

Request for more observation
```

Review remains outside the Planner.

---

```text
Evidence Records
```

The Planner should prefer validated cognition whenever available.

```text
Plan Candidates

```

These outputs remain non-operational.

---

# Truth Integration

Truth provides planning stability.

Without truth:

```text
Planning becomes speculation.
```

Truth anchors planning to validated cognition.

Every plan should reference supporting truths whenever possible.

---

# Risk Integration

Risk provides planning caution.

Without risk:

```text
Planning becomes reckless.
```

Risk allows the Planner to evaluate consequences before execution exists.

---

# Understanding Integration

Understanding provides meaning.

Without understanding:

```text
Planning becomes mechanical.
```

Understanding explains why a candidate may be valuable.

---

# Experience Integration

Experience provides historical context.

Future Planner implementations should consider:

```text
Previous outcomes

Repeated failures

Repeated successes

Observed patterns
```

Experience improves candidate quality over time.

---

# Habitat First Integration

Habitat First is the highest planning constraint.

When two candidates exist:

Candidate A:
higher optimization

Candidate B:
higher habitat safety

The Planner must prefer Candidate B.

Survival precedes optimization.

This rule is absolute.

---

# Planning Awareness

The Planner should eventually become aware of:

```text
Current objectives

Active candidates

Expired candidates

Rejected candidates

Knowledge gaps
```

Planning awareness improves explainability.

# Planning Expiration

Plans should not live forever.

Possible causes of expiration:

```text
Truth revision

Risk revision

Policy revision

Authority revision

Environmental change
```

Expired plans must not be considered active.

---

# Planning Revision

Plans may be revised.

Revision may occur when:

```text
New evidence appears

New truths emerge

Risk changes

Objectives change
```

Revision is a sign of healthy cognition.

---

# Planning Failure Modes

Potential planning failures include:

```text
Overconfidence

Single-path planning

Ignoring risk

Ignoring uncertainty

Ignoring habitat constraints

Dependency blindness
```

The Planner must continuously defend against these failures.

---

# Planning Explainability

Every plan should answer:

```text
Why does this exist?

What objective does it serve?

What evidence supports it?

Which truths support it?

Which risks oppose it?

Why is it ranked this way?
```

Unexplainable planning is invalid planning.

---

# Relationship With Governance

Planning is subordinate to governance.

Governance may reject any plan.

The Planner cannot override governance.

The Planner cannot grant authority.

The Planner cannot grant approval.

---

# Relationship With Execution

Planning precedes execution.

Execution consumes approved plans.

Planning does not perform execution.

This separation must remain permanent.

# Summary

The Planning Cycle transforms cognition into candidate futures.

It exists to explore possibilities before action.

The Planner reasons about objectives, risks, truths and constraints while remaining completely non-operational.

Planning is the bridge between understanding reality and responsibly changing reality.

The Planning Cycle therefore represents the first step from cognition toward future autonomy while remaining fully constrained by Habitat First governance.
